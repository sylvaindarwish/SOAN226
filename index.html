Coping with religious diversity: Incommensurability and other perspectives

Four models of "how different traditions relate to each other in a pluralistic setting":

  • mutual exclusion
  • commensurability as all contribute to the formation of a unified "system of meaning", "logic of action", or "religious identity".
  • market of religion
  • "pluralism"

"All these models share the assumption that different religious traditions can be easily translated into one another […] Religions are understood as being fully convertible […] because the analyst places them under a common description." (122)

Incommensurability:

  • Epistemologically: understanding the relation between two or more practices, views or moral statements without referring to "a third 'meta' point of reference, such as systems of meaning of the market." (122)
  • "incommensurability refers to a state in which two phenomena (or worlds) cannot be compared by a third without producing serious distortion." (Povinelli, quoted p. 123)
  • different ways of doing and seeing things standing neither with nor against each other (123)
  • Ethically: living with differences without trying to resolve them or looking for equivalents.

Famadihana - the "turning of the dead" ritual

To "turn the dead" means that the remains of the dead are taken out of the family tomb during the feast. (124)

Perspectives:

  • Catholic church: a Christian ritual and an expression of Malagasy culture
  • Lutheran and Reformed pastors: "traditional religion" and no mixing (of religions) is allowed + expenses (wink wink Weber) and alcohol consumption.
  • Revival and Pentecost Churches: a religious (demonic) ritual.

Participants:

  • Wrong ritual of a wrong religion
  • Unity of religious traditions (ancestors as intercessors between humans and the Christian God)
  • Two religions, but "simply something else"
  • "religion" is only Christianity, famdihana pertains to "family", "tradition", or "local politics"
